How Starbucks Came To Be

Coffee Culture

Starbucks  

• 2 minutes read

Let us open this edition with a fun fact: The word cappuccino, meaning ‘little hood’ entered the lexicon in 1948 . And another: The word Frappuccino® is a Starbucks trademark, an exclusive collection of iced coffee blended with syrups, toppings, and other ingredients to form exciting flavours like the Java Chip Frappuccino®.



With that, let us look into how these inventions found their place in the United States and elsewhere. 1966 . American coffee was still not in the best shape. In fact, Alfred Peet, the son of a Dutch coffee roaster, found the coffee to be of such poor quality that he started roasting his own — and soon opened Peet’s Coffee & Tea in Berkeley, California.



That’s when specialty coffee really took off in the United States. He developed a growing audience for dark-roasted coffee and mentored Jerry Baldwin, Zev Siegl, and Gordon Bowker in his roasting style. These three young men would become the founders of Starbucks.



Did You Know? The name ‘Starbucks’ is referenced from the first mate (Starbuck) in Melville’s novel Moby Dick.



In the year 1971 , these three young men started the first Starbucks store in Seattle’s Pike Place Market. The name evoked the romance of the high seas and the seafaring tradition of early coffee traders. In 1971 , Starbucks opened its first store in Seattle’s Pike Place Market. The small shop was revered for its fine coffee, friendly baristas, and unique experience. Designed to be a comfortable abode with soft music and a straightforward allure, the experience caught on.



Starbucks soon expanded in 1973 to establish the first roasting plant in Seattle. From there, Starbucks has constantly evolved with innovations that have allowed us to remain ahead of the curve. We’ve expanded to over 80 countries always striving to inspire and nurture the human spirit.



And the rest, as we say, is our history.
